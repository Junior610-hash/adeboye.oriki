Alake of Abeokuta

    Yoruba Oriki:
  • Ìjẹ̀bú ọmọ alárè, ọmọ awujálè,
  • Ọmọ arójò joyè,
  • Ọmọ alágemo Ògún woyowoyo,
  • Ọmọ aladìye ògògòmógà,
  • Ọmọ adìye bàlókùn omilili,
  • Ara òrokùn, Ara ò radìye,
  • Ọmọ ohùn ṣení òyòyònyò,
  • Òyòyò má yọmọ ohùn ṣení olèpàní,
  • Ọmọ dúdú ilé kò mọ ibi tí eṣú njẹ òṣì,
  • Pupa tọmọ ibi tí eṣú ku si inlẹ̀,
  • Ọmọ mọrẹ̀yè má mọ rokò, morokòtan ẹ̀yẹ...
    • English Meaning:
  • Ijebu,children of the owner of the are (a traditional cap/adornment),children of Awujale (the title of the king),
  • Children who find it easy to become a chief as one would walk in the rain,
  • Children of the chameleon of the god Ogun, who move with elegance (referencing the Agemo festival/deity),
  • Children of the chicken with a long, slender neck,
  • Children whose chicken can step on the ocean and cause ripples,
  • People of the sea/lagoon area, people of the (place where one) does not trade a chicken,
  • Children whose voice is sweet and sharp,
  • The sharp voice that does not know its owner can kill its owner (a warning against loose talk),
  • The dark-skinned child does not know where the locust is eating the corn,
  • The light-skinned child knows where the locust died,
  • Children who know wealth but do not know suffering; when suffering is known, it ceases to exist for them (a testament to their prosperity).

    Verses of Oriki Alake Abeokuta

    Yoruba Oriki (Continued):
  • Kelebe Ìjẹ̀bú owó, ìtò Ìjẹ̀bú owó, Dúdú Ìjẹ̀bú owó, Pupa Ìjẹ̀bú owó.
  • Ọmọ àbá méjì dínlógún, tí wọ́n fìlù kan dáyé gbọ̀ngbọ̀ngbọ̀n,
  • Ọmọ Onírẹ́mo, Ọmọ Owòsì, Ọmọ Olúkòkù.
    • Awujale, agbe 'gi l'ejo, Olokun, meji okun sun,
  • Alawujale, aforan f'ejo d'ola.
  • Agbani tan r'osan, r'osan a r'oru.
  • Ọmọ Ọmùsù, Ọmọ Ọmùsù Àpará, Ibi tí erin fìtì, tí efon fìtì, Tí ahọ̀n ọmọ ènìyàn kò le fìtì.
    • Ìjẹ̀bú, ẹ̀yẹ tí í fò lókè ìjù, tí í fò lókè igbó,
  • Tí í fi apá kan bọ́ra, tí í fi apá kejì bọ́mo.
  • Ìjẹ̀bú rere, ọmọ òṣù gbẹndègbẹndè lórí ẹ̀sọ́.
    • English Meaning (Continued):
  • The small Ijebu is money, the tall Ijebu is money, the black Ijebu is money, the light-skinned Ijebu is money
    • (emphasizing their historical association with wealth and commerce).
  • Children of the sixteen councils (elders/chiefs), who with one voice/unanimity established the world soundly,
  • Children of Oniremo, Children of Owosi, Children of Olukoku (ancestral names/titles).
  • Awujale, who uses wood to stop a case/dispute (resolves conflicts definitively), Owner of the ocean, who sleeps in the middle of the sea,
  • Alawujale, who turns a case/dispute into honor/wealth.
  • One who helps you resolve a matter completely, whether in the afternoon or at night.
  • Children of Omushu, Children of Omushu Apara (a specific lineage/place name),
  • Where the elephant stumbled, where the buffalo stumbled,
  • But where the tongue of a human being cannot stumble (a reference to their careful speech and wisdom).
  • Ijebu, the bird that flies over the wilderness, that flies over the forest,
  • That covers itself with one wing, and covers its child with the other wing (a metaphor for protection and care).
  • Good Ijebu, children of the grand, flowing headdress worn by guards/warriors.
